Get Your Content Right

There’s a strong impulse when you’re preparing your slides to include too much content. Research has shown that people typically remember only four slides from a twenty-page deck.∗ That’s not very encouraging news if you’re putting your heart and soul into an informative presentation, but from a strategic point of view, it’s good to know. Rather than packing your presentation full of facts, you’re better off choosing a few key points you want your audience to remember, and organizing the presentation around those. Think of your PowerPoint deck as a set of prompts for your performance rather than as a repository for complete information.

Set up your slides as a visual aid for when you’re making a speech or presentation, not as a trove of data. When presented with a very text-heavy slide, people will typically space out or stop listening and read the slide (people can read faster than you can talk). If you want to provide detailed information to your audience, you can make and distribute a leave-behind deck that contains your entire talk. For the presentation itself, keep your slides concise and the focus on you.

To put the information about proposals in context, we can consider a case study. 4F&D, Inc. is a fictional company that operates a series of local food delivery services under the brand Fork Over: Fast and Free Food Delivery.

Based in Chicago, Illinois, 4F&D, Inc. was founded by Amy Owner in 2008. The company jumped into the crowded food-delivery field, where the main competitors were individual restaurants that hired their own delivery people to cover a specific territory. 4F&D launched Fork Over: Fast and Free Food Delivery in the fall of 2009 serving 25 restaurants. 4F&D provided client restaurants with both a technology system to facilitate online orders, including processing credit cards, and a third-party delivery service in exchange for a commission for each sale. While restaurants were skeptical at first, they also saw that they had little to lose. The commission was on sales that they would otherwise not have made. Writer’s Sidebar: Avoid Unclear Construction

An unclear construction can occur when the subject is unclear, when there are potentially multiple subjects, when there is an unclear shift from a singular to a plural, or, at times, when there is a weakness in the logical connection between clauses.

Example:The PTA and the school both have deep interests: They voted in favor of the new soda machine.

Revision: The PTA and the school have competing interests: While the school voted in favor of a new soda machine, the PTA voted against it.

Example: The reason the car broke down is because the oil is leaking.

Revision: The car broke down because there is a crack in the oil pan and all of the oil leaked out.

Language clarity is the key in communicating with a reader. Writers want to establish direct cause and effect relationships, or otherwise explain the logic behind an issue clearly and systematically. Writers also want to avoid unclear referents, such as in the case of the first example, in which the referent “They” was unclear. Whomever or whatever you are referring to should always be obvious. Make sure that you ascribe the action of the sentence to one single subject.

Here, we have two possible subjects, the PTA and the school. Either of these two groups could have voted in favor of the soda machine, but the sentence does not clarify which did the voting.
Using “the reason” and “because” is on sentence should be avoided because it is repetitive. Also, there is a lack of logical clarity in this particular sentence. Is the reason why the car broke down that the oil is leaking? Why is the oil leaking? That is the real “reason” for the problem.
