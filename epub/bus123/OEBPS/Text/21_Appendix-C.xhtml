Appendix C: Finding a Career

Job searching can be a job in itself. It takes spending time, undertaking market and company research, organizing the information you find, preparing résumés and cover letters, going on job interviews, writing thank-you letters for those interviews, and then repeating the process—often many times—before you are hired. This appendix is intended to remind you of some best practices when searching for a career and the numerous resources you have at your disposal.

One of the most valuable (and free) resources you, as an Ashford student and, later, an alumnus, have access to is My Career. There, you can access a wealth of information and obtain one-on-one help from a Career Advisor by e-mail or phone. My Career contains resources that include a résumé builder, résumé templates, mock interview modules, and individualized career pathways based on your degree program. The site also includes resources for career exploration, self-assessment, and job searches, as well as special resources for military students. Log on to My Career through either your Student Portal or the Student Support Center in your online classroom.

Don’t be discouraged if the ideal job does not come along as quickly as you hope. Always remember that career management is an important competency that you should work to develop—career management doesn’t end when you secure your first job after graduation. Other important competencies include oral/written communication and professionalism/work ethic. Each time you apply for a job, optimize your résumé, or craft a cover letter, you are demonstrating your mastery of these competencies, which, in turn, increases your ability to secure an ideal position.

Job Searching

You should always research a company before you apply to work there—this will help you learn as much as you can about the organization. The company’s website is one of your best sources of information. Through My Career, Ashford students also have premium access to Vault.com. Vault is a fantastic resource for researching companies, industries, and job market trends. General job and career websites and job boards can be useful resources as well. The following table describes and evaluates some of the most popular job boards. Just keep in mind that not all jobs will be listed on job boards.

It’s important to remember that although the digital world makes it easy to submit applications for jobs online, you will have far better success getting your foot in the door through networking than by applying randomly for jobs you find on the web. While simply responding to job postings can work, it is often like cold calling or telemarketing. Sometimes you can have success, but you will probably encounter more rejection than you will if someone who knows you introduces you to a potential employer or puts in a good word for you with a company where he or she currently works.

www.indeed.com

Indeed lists millions of jobs, in the United States and globally, compiled from company websites, staffing firms, other job boards, and newspapers.

You can search for jobs by job title, key words, or company name and by city or state. You can also post your résumé here.

Posting your own résumé on a job board such as this one may work against you. You may get a reputation as a “spammer.” Employers and recruiters tire of seeing the same résumés on different job boards. You also may encounter privacy issues—your résumé could be seen by your current employer!

You might be better advised to use this site to point you to company websites, where you can apply for the jobs directly.

www.usajobs.gov

USAJobs lists all available federal government jobs.

The site is straightforward and includes a wide range of job postings from around the country.

You must follow exact instructions to be considered for any job listed on this site.

It’s a good place to get notices of jobs, but applying for a government job requires specific expertise. Contact Ashford University Career Services, through My Career, to schedule an appointment with a certified federal job search trainer and coach.

www.glassdoor.com

Founded in 2007, Glassdoor offers a transparent look inside companies.

The site includes salary reports, interview questions reports, and company reviews to give job seekers an insider view of the company.

The job listings are relatively few, and the company profiles are uneven, as dissatisfied workers tend to be more vocal than those who are satisfied.

It’s a good place to gather information about potential hirers, but it may be limited in scope.

www.idealist.org

Founded in 1995, this is the primary job board for the volunteer and nonprofit sector.

The site charges organizations a minimal fee for posting jobs but is the recognized leader in serving this sector. It hosts job fairs for colleges and graduate programs.

Postings are aimed primarily at lower-paying and entry-level positions.

Working in the nonprofit sector and volunteering are good ways to begin building a résumé and workplace skills, but stable, midlevel jobs are scarce and do not have much turnover.

www.dhigroupinc.com

DHI Group matches employers and candidates in six specialized fields and provides industry news in those fields (energy, technology and security clearance professions, financial services, health care, and hospitality industries).

DHI is a global company that works with recruiters, employers, and job candidates to provide connections within these specialized professions.

Job postings range from entry-level to highly specialized occupations in the industries it covers. DHI works with all parties to provide the right fit for employer and employee.

Specialized services are available only in the professions listed. DHI acts as a third party, so it prescreens candidates before they talk with hiring managers.

www.mediabistro.com

Founded in 1997, Mediabistro has been the leading site for all things media, including jobs, educational programs, and networking events.

This is the primary source for jobs in the media industry, including publishing, broadcast, and marketing.

Competition is very stiff for media jobs, and this site ensures that each job post will attract a high volume of responses.

If you want to work in media, this is a good place to learn about a range of career opportunities. Job posts are best suited for lower and midlevel positions.

www.linkedin.com

LinkedIn is the leading social network for professionals and career development.

You can create job alerts based on geographic location, hard and soft skills, and preferred industries.

Success with this network is mainly dependent on the optimization of your LinkedIn profile.

LinkedIn is a premier job board site, but you’ll need to optimize your profile. Check out the following resource for tips: LinkedIn Overview and Best Practices.

Networking

"Networking is the process whereby you get connected to and build relationships with people who can help advance your career" (Hansen, 2008, p. 7).∗ In A Foot in the Door: Networking Your Way into a Hidden Job Market, Hansen divides networking into two basic types: developmental and strategic. Developmental networking occurs as you make connections with people you know from various social, work, recreational, and service aspects of your life. Developmental networking grows as people get to know you as a responsible, dependable individual who fulfills commitments and is working toward specific goals. This type of networking occurs as the result of social contacts and organizational affiliations.

Strategic networking is networking with a purpose: finding and securing employment that allows entry into and progression within a specific career. Friends, family, course instructors, former employers, internship supervisors and coworkers, and the people you have interviewed or shadowed form the basis of your strategic network. This type of intentional networking takes time. It is never too soon to begin to make connections with people you have met through professional organizations or those currently employed in the companies you have targeted for possible employment.

Of course, not everyone you meet has the hiring power to advance your career, but often your contacts can be an intermediate link to valuable information and career advice while steering you toward opportunities to meet the people who actually do the hiring. The number one rule of networking is never to overlook the role of well-networked intermediaries. They can provide the word-of-mouth recommendation that results in your résumé receiving a second look or your application a positive review. The number two rule is closely related to the first: you are more likely to be hired if you are referred by a mutual acquaintance than if you submit the application or résumé "cold" (Hansen, 2008, p. 9).

Networking with other peers and professionals is one of the best ways to hear about job openings before they are posted on job boards. Finding a suitable position generally requires more than applying for a job online. If you want to get noticed and learn about opportunities before they are ever posted online, you have to network. While it may seem daunting, it is likely something you do every day without even realizing it. For example, getting to know your instructors and fellow classmates helps you build your professional network.

Networking shouldn’t end when you find a suitable position. Building professional relationships affects your career trajectory, supports your collaborations with other professionals in your field, and can help you develop key insights or expertise. Think of networking as just that—relationship-building. Look for industry-specific meetups in your region, join a student organization, or become a member of a professional organization or society. These are all ways to expand and enrich your personal network.

On Social Media

Social and professional networking websites can be excellent ways to connect with people who are working in fields you would like to enter or who know people you might contact for more information or professional opportunities in a specific field. By networking on LinkedIn, for example, you can find out who is hiring and get leads from other people in their network. The website includes professionals who want to find clients who might use their skills and job hunters who are looking for employment. Recruiters who are responsible for hiring people at their companies also use LinkedIn to find potential employees.

To use LinkedIn, you must create an account on the website and make a public profile to use the job search features. The networking features are easy to use and allow you to connect directly with other members. LinkedIn has become an active and popular space to form specific networks for yourself, such as joining a network of other Ashford students or one based on your job interests or experiences. At Job Fairs

Don’t forget, too, to check out job fairs or career fairs for networking opportunities. Throughout the country, such fairs are held regularly (usually at no charge) by large companies, groups of employers, local communities, government agencies, school districts, and other organizations to find qualified job candidates. These events usually last for a few hours or half a day at a specific location. Because different employers are often present, you usually will not be able to customize your résumé to a specific organization. However, if you wish, you will have the chance to meet a number of different company representatives, chat with them briefly, and hand them a copy of a general résumé you have prepared.

To participate in these job or career fairs, search using the keywords “job fair” or “career fair” and the name of your local community or state for calendars of nearby events. You can also check out one of the following job fair websites:

Informational Interviewing

An informational interview (sometimes called a "career conversation") allows you to talk with a person who is currently employed in a job in your field of interest. It provides an opportunity to gather information about a career from a real insider. By meeting with someone who has first-hand experience, you become more fully aware of the daily responsibilities and challenges of the job, and you gain perspective about the realities of the career from someone who is living it. People who request informational interviews want knowledge and information about real-life experiences beyond what they have read in a book or heard about from a career counselor. The informational interview allows you to determine whether a specific career might be a good fit for you.

Finding the right person for the informational interview is key. Contact family members, friends, friends of friends, or individuals you have read about in local business news publications to help you identify the best candidate for the interview. If you are enrolled in courses, communicate with your instructors in order to gain their expertise and input. Ask if they know of someone you could reach out to. Keep your interactions professional; the contacts you make during this process help lay the groundwork for future employment, as you'll be building a network of people who know that you are actively seeking a career in a specific area.

Once you have secured names, request appointments with these people, clarifying with each that the purpose is to conduct an informational interview. Once the appointments are scheduled, begin to gather information about the role each of your interviewees plays within his or her organization, as well as information about the organization for which each works. The informational interviews can provide you with "dress rehearsals" for actual job interviews: you must dress and act the part of a professional job seeker, and for both, you must do your homework to learn about the field, the organization, and the specific job position.
