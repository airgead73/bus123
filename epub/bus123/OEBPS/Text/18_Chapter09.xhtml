Writing With a Team

Many forms of business communication are part of a collaborative writing process, where multiple individuals work together to design, create, and produce the final product. While collaboration in writing is hardly new, what has changed is how both executives and employees view collaborative writing: The emphasis has shifted from evaluating the end product to assessing the process used to produce it. You can be a talented writer, but if you are writing with others and fail to communicate and collaborate effectively, the resulting piece will read poorly.

Successful collaborative writing generally occurs in four stages:

1.Define the mission

2.Create the action plan

3.Establish goals and deadlines

4.Finalize the project

Ideally, your collaborative writing timelines will tend to look like an inverted hourglass: brief amounts of time are spent defining the mission (stage 1) and finalizing the project (stage 4), and most time is given to creating the action plan (stage 2) and establishing goals and deadlines (stage 3). The two middle stages are the site of the kind of creative chaos that often generates innovative thinking—which is one of the primary advantages of collaboration.

9.1 Stage 1: Define the Mission

Before any collaborative project can begin, the team must first define the mission. A mission statement is an essential tool that provides clear directions about the scope of a project and the purpose of the team. The mission serves two primary purposes. First, it should be the only part of the collaboration that, once written and agreed upon, does not change. Second, it should be the frame of reference the group uses for every step of the process. As the team works on various aspects of the project, every member should review the mission statement to make sure the parts align with the mission statement.

As an example, let’s consider Team Outreach, which has been asked to create a proposal in a month that describes how to establish stronger local loyalty to the Planet Plants brand. The team of 10 will be working remotely to ensure representation from urban areas, small towns, mid-sized cities, and various key regions in the United States. You can read their mission statement and action plan at the end of this section.

The mission statement should not take more than a single meeting to generate ideas and a portion of another meeting to accept a final version. The second part of defining the mission is to create a viable timeline for each stage of the project, identifying how much time is required for the next three stages.

9.2 Stages 2 and 3: Create an Action Plan and Establish Goals and Deadlines

With a mission statement to guide the process, Team Outreach can now develop an action plan that lays out the various stages of development, what they entail, which members will participate throughout the process, and how they will participate. If the mission statement is set in stone, the action plan is the part of the collaborative process that allows for the most flexibility.

It is useful to examine the various options that are used in collaborative writing. There are five collaborative writing strategies based on the findings of Paul Benjamin Lowry, Aaron Curtis, and Michelle René Lowry (“Building a Taxonomy and Nomenclature of Collaborative Writing to Improve Interdisciplinary Research and Practice”).∗ The role that authorship plays in collaborative documents very much depends upon the autonomy of the writer at various stages in the process.

Collaborative writing type

Description

Single-author

Occurs when one team member writes as a representative for the entire team. Single-author writing usually happens when the writing task is simple.

Sequential single

One group member writes at a time. Each group member is assigned a portion of the document, writes his or her portion, and then passes the document onto the next group member.

Parallel

A type of collaborative writing that occurs when a group divides the assignment or document into separate parts, and all members work on their assigned part at the same time. There are two types of parallel writing: Horizontal division parallel writing occurs when group members divide the task into sections, each member being responsible for the development of his or her assigned section. Stratified division parallel writing occurs when group members divide responsibility of the creation of the product by assigning different members different roles. Some examples of roles that a member could be assigned are author, editor, facilitator, or team leader.

Reactive

Occurs when team members collaborate synchronously to develop their product. Team members react to and adjust each other’s contributions as they are made.

Mixed mode

Describes a form of writing that mixes two or more of the collaborative writing strategies described above.

While there is no empirical evidence for which strategy is used most in business collaborative writing, the mixed mode is probably the most common as more tools are becoming available. A major consideration in reviewing these options is that the type of strategy will change as the document progresses. For instance, parallel writing might be ideal for getting the various sections of the document started, while single-author writing might be the best option for creating a master rough draft.

E-mail and collaborative editing tools such as Google Docs reduce the need for face/voice time when working on written documents. This leaves the bulk of time for information and ideas generation and, later, the critical assessment stage, where content and order are finalized.

Again, the planning stage of the process should be fluid. The point is to generate as much valuable content as possible. The advantage to this approach is that the team can experience the equivalent of bouncing ideas off one another. One team member might be working on parks and recreation and be inspired by someone else who is working on green revitalization.

9.3 Stage 4: Finalize the Project

The final stage is not time consuming for the group. While the team may have a chance to participate in collaborative revisions, at some point the revision process stops and the final editing stage begins.
