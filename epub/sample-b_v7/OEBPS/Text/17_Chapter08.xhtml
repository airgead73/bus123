Persuasive Writing

Much of the time when we communicate—either through speech or through writing—we are trying to persuade. Think about these situations:

We want our boss to give us a raise.

We want a friend to join us at Starbucks.

We want a potential voter to agree with our candidate.

We want a consumer to buy our product.

We want a legislator to concur with our position.

We want a certain someone to go out with us.

All of these are examples of trying to persuade someone about something.

We have been exposed to and practiced persuasion all our lives. As infants, we learned that crying was a successful way to get our needs met. As we became adults, our needs became increasingly complex, and so did the strategies we used to persuade others to meet them. One way to persuade is through writing. You may be assigned persuasive and argumentative papers in your college courses. You may need to put together a cover letter or résumé. You may have to write a persuasive e-mail or proposal. Regardless, when writing persuasively, your objective is to promote action. You want the person receiving your message to adopt your position, buy your product, endorse your nomination, and so on.

When writing persuasively, you must first consider the following:

1.Whom are you attempting to persuade? Who is your target audience? What is its makeup? What do your audience members believe now about what you’re trying to persuade them to think or do? What predispositions do they bring to the argument? What about this argument concerns them? What do they like or dislike about it? What are the important issues for this group? For example, if a CEO is addressing employees on what they must do to succeed, the audience is solidly “with” the speaker in wishing to achieve the desired goal. On the other hand, if the CEO addresses a hostile committee of Congress, the audience is much more skeptical. Knowing the audience you’re writing for before you start is essential.

2.What is the essence of your argument? What is your thesis, your central idea, the heart of what you are trying to persuade people to do? Clearly conceiving your main argument may take time, but it is worth the struggle so that you can clearly articulate what you want people to do and why it is in their best interests.

3.What do you have to back up your argument? Do you have facts to back up your point of view? Statistics? Examples? Illustrations? Anecdotes? Experience? It is, of course, critical to have passion for your argument—but passion can only go so far. You must have logical evidence to back up your arguments so that even skeptics must pay attention.

4.What are the key messages to corroborate your argument? Once you have gathered your supporting material, you are ready to craft the key messages that will add heft to your main argument. These should be written as creatively and provocatively as possible in order to be remembered.

5.What are viable alternatives to your position? Finally, in framing your argument and backing it up, you must also consider the other options or alternatives that may be available if your position is not adopted. These alternative ideas are, by definition, inferior to your position—else why would anyone adopt your persuasive argument? You must refute these arguments in a rational way so that objective observers can see that your position is best.

8.1 Three Means of Persuasion

In Rhetoric, Aristotle (350 BCE) identified three different methods or means of persuasion. He suggested that we can try to persuade based on the character of the person making the persuasive appeal (ethos), through emotion (pathos), or through logic (logos).

For example, suppose your dentist says, "You need to floss your teeth every day and brush twice a day. I'm a dentist, so I should know." She is using her credentials to persuade you to adopt better dental hygiene. However, if your dentist says, "Brush your teeth—it will make you feel good," she is appealing to your emotions. Finally, if she says, "Flossing your teeth once a day and brushing twice a day has been proven to reduce cavities," and then hands you a brochure about the findings of a study on flossing, she is using logic to persuade you.

Appealing to the Character of the Writer

The primary reason advertisers hire famous athletes or celebrities to endorse their products is that audiences think they have credibility. Basketball superstar Michael Jordan was a credible spokesperson for athletic shoes because he was a great athlete, audiences believed he knew a lot about sneakers, and they trusted him to tell them the truth. Celebrities who become embroiled in scandals often lose the public's trust and, correspondingly, their lucrative product endorsement deals. Losing credibility diminishes the effectiveness of any persuasive message.

To have credibility as a writer, your readers must perceive you as knowledgeable, believable, trustworthy, and qualified to write on the subject. If your topic is something you have experienced personally or are an expert about, state this in your writing; it helps establish your credibility. However, you need not have personal knowledge or be an expert on a subject to write about it. You can support your statements by conducting research and using evidence to back up what you say. Evidence consists of personal examples, facts, statistics, or quotations from credible authorities or other believable sources. Opinions are not legitimate pieces of evidence. Anyone can state an opinion. However, if a writer supports an idea with careful evidence, it is no longer an opinion but a supported interpretation. Make sure you supply supporting evidence whenever you state your opinion or are not an expert on a subject. Also make sure to give credit to any sources you consult.

Appealing to Emotion

To be persuasive, we must often be emotional—that is, we must touch people's hearts as well as their minds. People's feelings influence their actions. Some common emotional appeals are listed here. As you read this list, think of advertisements you have encountered that feature these appeals.

Fear. Appeals to emotion frequently involve fear—specifically, the fear of losing family members, a home, or belongings due to theft or natural disaster; becoming seriously ill or injured; being assaulted; being injured in a traffic accident; being rejected or disliked; or experiencing economic hardship or losing a job.

Compassion. Requests to contribute to earthquake victims, victims of domestic violence, abused or neglected animals, starving children, and other such causes tap into humans' capacity for compassion.

Pride. People's sense of pride in their country, family, community, ethnic heritage, or personal accomplishments can motivate them to take action. This appeal is often coupled with the appeal to self-gratification—for example, the idea that "You deserve it!" or "You've earned it!"

Anger. Anger can be directed toward anyone—for example, suspected terrorists, government officials, business executives who receive large bonuses when their companies are in trouble, or thieves and other criminals.

Shame. People often feel ashamed of themselves for not helping people less fortunate, for having material possessions while others go without, for not doing their best, for not living up to others' expectations, or for not conforming to ideals set by their families or religious teachings.

Admiration or love of others. Many emotional appeals are aimed at the basic human need to be liked, be admired, find love, and belong. We humans are social creatures, and others' opinions of us can serve as a powerful motivator.

People have many emotions, so this list is not all-inclusive. Keep in mind that people are often persuaded to act in ways they think are in their best interest and that meet their own needs. Appealing to your readers' emotions can engage them in an issue and show them how their actions will personally benefit them. This can be a powerful way to obtain support for the ideas you propose.

One way to incorporate emotional appeals in persuasive writing is to use rhetorical devices, which aim to achieve a particular effect. Rhetorical devices can help capture and hold a reader's attention and improve the impact of a writer's statements. They can be used to help a reader imagine a scene or picture specific details. The following table lists some examples of common rhetorical devices that can improve persuasive writing, when used sparingly.

Rhetorical device

Example

Emotive language

How can we expect a broken, penniless, and desperate man to find a job?

Exaggeration

Everything is always completely wrong.

Metaphor (an implied comparison between two things)

Her smile was sunshine in my dreary world.

Simile (a comparison between two things that uses the words "like" or "as")

Her smile was like a ray of sunshine in my dreary world.

Irony (a figure of speech that illustrates a contrast between reality and appearance)

It was hard to believe, but he was absent on the day he was to receive his award for perfect attendance.

Alliteration (repetition of initial consonants in words in a series)

Careful and cost-efficient collaboration can ensure company success.

Antithesis (placing two opposing ideas together in a sentence)

Neil Armstrong's famous words upon landing on the moon: "That's one small step for a man, one giant leap for mankind."

These devices should subtly imply a point; be careful not to overuse a rhetorical device. The following sentence illustrates why:

Peter's report fails to persuade the people who oppose his perspective because his points lack punch and the paragraphs lack pizzazz.

This writer uses so much alliteration that, by the end, the text reads almost comically rather than genuinely. Take care not to overuse any tone or writing technique—doing so can compromise the piece's seriousness or credibility.

Appealing to Reason

You may be a credible voice on a topic, and you may skillfully feature emotion in your writing. However, if what you write does not make sense or is unreasonable, you will not win over your readers. This is why reason is an important component in persuasive writing. The use of reason and analysis can be very convincing. Before readers can be persuaded to change their thoughts, they must believe what you say makes sense, is factual, and sounds reasonable.

Suppose you want to persuade people to exercise to improve their health. You might support your point of view with the pieces of evidence in the following table.

Evidence

Example

Explanation

Experience

"By exercising thirty minutes each day, I have lowered my blood pressure to the point where I no longer have to take daily blood pressure medication."

This individualized example takes the subject of health and exercise from the objective realm of science and makes it personal. The statement enhances your credibility and perhaps increases the likelihood that readers will accept your opinion on the subject. However, this evidence proves only that exercising worked to lower your blood pressure; your story does not prove that everyone who exercises will experience the same results.

Facts (statements that are not in dispute or that can be proven true by reasoning, experimentation, or personal experience)

"Exercise is beneficial for your physical and mental health."

The writer is stating a fact that is not in dispute and has been verified by research. Because it is common knowledge, no source is needed. You obtain facts from observing, reading, researching, or experiencing the world around you.

Statistics, or data that can be represented numerically

"Childhood obesity is on the rise in the United States. Between 1976 and 1980, approximately 5% of youth 2 to 19 years of age were obese. In 2006, the rate had increased to 16.3%" (Dietz, 2009, para. 5).

The writer uses data offering percentages from different points in time to support the statement that childhood obesity rates are on the rise. Statistics provide excellent support for your statements because they are specific and concrete.

Statements from authorities or experts

"In early 2010, First Lady Michelle Obama, U.S. Health and Human Services Secretary Kathleen Sebelius, and Surgeon General Dr. Regina Benjamin said that childhood obesity had reached record heights and constituted the number one health threat facing children" (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2010).

The writer identifies the source of authority and then paraphrases a statement made about childhood obesity. Expert statements may also be direct quotations. In both cases, you must cite the source.

When writing persuasively, you also must take care to avoid using logical fallacies in making your argument. A logical fallacy is an error or flaw in reasoning. Although fallacious arguments seem plausible and often are quite persuasive, they are essentially unsound. Fallacies are not always a deliberate effort to deceive readers, but because they are so easily constructed, we have to be aware of what they are and how to avoid them in our own writing. Writers may inadvertently introduce a fallacy into their writing by not having solid support, using ambiguous words, or even not examining their own reasons and understanding of the topic. The following table offers a summary of the most common logical fallacies and examples of each.

Logical fallacy

Example

Explanation

Faulty cause: Mistakes correlation for causation. This fallacy assumes that because one thing follows another, the latter was caused by the former.

The introduction of sex education courses at the high school level has resulted in increased promiscuity among teens. A recent study revealed that the number of reported cases of sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) was significantly higher for high schools that offered courses in sex education than for high schools that did not.

The example incorrectly connects sex education and the higher incidence of STDs; more information is needed before a causal relationship can be determined.

Hasty generalization: Bases an inference on too small a sample, or on an unrepresentative sample. Often, a single example or instance is used as the basis for a broader generalization.

All movie stars are really rude. I asked (insert celebrity's name here) for her autograph in a restaurant the other evening, and she told me to get lost.

A hasty generalization was made when the person assumed that because one movie star was rude, all of them are rude.

Faulty analogy: Can be literal or figurative; assumes that because two things, events, or situations are alike in some known respects, they are alike in other unknown respects.

People who need a cup of coffee every morning before they can function have the same kind of problem as alcoholics who need their alcohol each day to sustain them.

The analogy is faulty because it asserts that coffee drinkers have a problem comparable to that of alcoholics who are chemically dependent on alcohol.

Equivocation: Allows a key word or term in an argument to shift its meaning during the course of the argument. The result is that the conclusion of the argument is not concerned with the same thing as the premise(s).

Only man is rational. No woman is a man. Therefore, no woman is rational.

In this example, the word “man” shifts meaning: it refers to all humans in the first usage but only males in the second usage. The conclusion does not stem from the same premise.

Appeal to authority: Attempts to justify an argument by citing a highly admired or well-known (but not necessarily qualified) figure who supports the conclusion being offered.

If it’s good enough for (insert celebrity’s name here), it’s good enough for me.

A celebrity might not be a valid source of information and knowledge. In this case, the writer is relying on the audience to accept a weak claim based on celebrity popularity.

Straw man: Stating an opponent’s argument in an extreme or exaggerated form, or attacking a weaker, irrelevant portion of an opponent’s argument.

What woman in her right mind could truly desire total equality with men? No woman wants the right to be shot at in times of war, the right to have to pay alimony, or the right to have to use the same restrooms as men.

In this case, the argument for gender equality is exaggerated to the point of absurdity.

Slippery slope: Sometimes called a snowball argument or domino theory; suggests that if one step or action is taken it will invariably lead to similar steps or actions, the end results of which are negative or undesirable. A slippery slope always assumes a chain reaction of cause-effect events that result in some eventual dire outcome.

If I let one student interrupt my lecture with a question, then I’ll have to let others and, before long, there won’t be any time left for my lecture.

In this example, the end result is exaggerated and unlikely, and it presents a falsely correlated series of events.

Non sequitur: An argument that fails to establish a connection between the premise and the conclusion. In practice, however, the label "non sequitur" tends to be reserved for arguments in which irrelevant reasons are offered to support a claim.

The one and only time I had eggplant, it was disgusting. Therefore, eggplant is bad for you and provides little nutrition.

It does not follow that eggplant is bad for you and provides little nutrition merely because you did not enjoy it the one and only time you had it.

In the following argument, a logical fallacy is used as false support. After reading the example, determine which logical fallacy is used:

Rhonda and Sam are on their way to the bowling alley one Saturday afternoon. It is hot outside, and Sam is thirsty. When they arrive, Sam goes to the snack bar to get a drink. Rhonda advises Sam to buy a bottle of water because it is a healthy and economical choice. Sam replies, “Pepsi is better! I saw a commercial with Beyoncé drinking Pepsi, so it has to be good.”

Which logical fallacy did Sam use in trying to convince Rhonda that soda is just as good as water? If you guessed appeal to authority, you are correct. Sam tries to prove he is right in choosing Pepsi over water by pointing out that Beyoncé endorses Pepsi. So, even if his choice is not healthier than water, he is persuaded by a celebrity’s connection to the soft drink. Majority rules in Sam’s mind.

8.2 Thinking About Tone and Language

The words we choose to express our attitudes and opinions say a lot about the intelligence of our arguments and the persuasiveness of our writing. Be sure to choose your words carefully. Bear in mind that different audiences will be persuaded by different language. For example, some words are politically sensitive. Terms like "capital punishment," "right-winger," "liberal," or "entitlement" are especially charged, depending on the audience. Knowing the words that can persuade your particular audience is critical.

To be truly persuasive, writers must think about their readers, imagining a typical recipient who needs to be persuaded and remembering to write in plain English.

8.3 Final Tips

Persuasive writing—writing in which you want to convince readers, listeners, or viewers to agree with your opinions—isn’t easy. It requires thought, practice, and passion, especially the last. You may ultimately disagree with the point of view of a really good persuasive writer, but you can’t doubt his or her sincerity or passion. A persuasive writer must also present content that invites people to read. In an Internet era in which millions publish their own blogs, the more shareable the content, the more readers are reached and potentially persuaded. The following types of content tend to work best:

Lists. People love lists. They will click on articles such as “Top 10 Tips for Producing YouTube Videos” or “10 Powerful Ways to Enhance Facebook Fan Engagement” or “Five Devices to Make Your Writing More Persuasive.”

Negative stories. Sorry to say it, but bad news works. People enjoy the negative angle, particularly when it concerns someone else. That’s why negative political advertising is so persuasive. Adopt a negative angle, and people will read.

How-to stories. People also appreciate simple, step-by-step instructions that may help them master a difficult concept or activity. Explaining how things work will persuade many to listen.

Case studies. By the same token, people also appreciate examples that work in similar organizations or industries. If you can learn what worked someplace else, you can save time in coming up with a likely solution.

Timely news. People want to know what’s going on—in their organization, industry, region, or world. If you provide commentary, advice, or knowledge that is related to what’s happening in a timely manner, people will be interested.

The simple always triumphs over the complex. And when the concepts are complex, consider using infographics, videos, and other media to make your content easier to decipher and more accessible. The most effective persuasive writing is that which exhibits logic and passion in a fresh and fascinating manner while taking advantage of the methods of content delivery that regularly attract a large audience.
